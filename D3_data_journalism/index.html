Correlations Discovered

Healthcare and Poverty

As you would expect, states with higher poverty levels have higher uninsured rates. This is particularly pronounced in the Deep South and the Southwest. Meanwhile, only 4.6% of Massachusetts residents are uninsured, by far the lowest of any state. That may be due to the implementation of "RomneyCare" in 2006. (This dataset is from 2014, when its nationwide cousin "ObamaCare" had only just been fully implemented.)

Obesity and Income

The low-income states in the Deep South and Mountain South also have the highest levels of obesity, followed by a cluster of states in the Midwest. Coastal states tend to have higher median income and lower obesity, but the state with slimmest population is landlocked: Coloradoans are famously outdoorsy, which probably explains their outlier status.

Age and Smoking

The correlations here are fairly week, but it's notable that the notoriously vice-averse citizens of Utah have by far the lowest smoking rates and median age. The Mountain South, which trends relatively older, has the highest smoking rates, with West Virginia leading the pack (no pun intended). Somewhat surprising is that North Carolina, home to Winston-Salem, bucks this trend. Perhaps higher rates in its Appalachian west are offset by lower rates in its major metropolis (Charlotte) and the Research Triangle.
